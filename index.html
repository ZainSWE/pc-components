About: Why choose parts?

Computers can come in many different shapes and sizes, which can be a double edged sword in the world of pc building. So to help, I've created a website that allows you to browse some of the latest pc parts available in the market.

Guide: What parts do I pick?

Before you go on to browse/buy different parts, it's imperative that you understand what each part does and how it can affect your overall pc build. Below you will find the most common parts for building a computer that follows recent standards and needs of certain components, rather than getting overloaded with information about outdated hardware.

  • Motherboard

    The motherboard is the main circuit board that connects every part of your PC. It decides what CPU and RAM types you can use, how many M.2 SSDs and PCIe cards you can install, and how power is delivered to the components. Today look for the right CPU socket, DDR5 support, enough M.2/PCIe lanes, and solid VRMs for reliability and future upgrades.

  • CPU (Central Processing Unit)

    The CPU is the brain of the computer. It runs programs and handles most calculations. Modern CPUs have multiple cores and threads, and higher single-core speed (IPC/boost) helps in everyday tasks and games. Some CPUs include integrated graphics for basic displays without a separate GPU.

  • GPU (Graphics Processing Unit)

    The GPU renders images, videos, and 3D graphics. It matters most for gaming, video editing, and AI-related tasks. Modern GPUs have dedicated memory (VRAM), support hardware-accelerated ray tracing, and use features like upscaling (AI image reconstruction) to boost performance.

  • RAM (Random Access Memory)

    RAM holds the data programs use right now. More RAM lets you run more apps at once and handle larger files. Speed and latency matter, and DDR5 is common today. Pick enough capacity (16GB+ for most users, 32GB+ for heavy multitasking or content creation).

  • SSD (Solid State Drive)

    SSDs store your files and the operating system. NVMe PCIe SSDs are much faster than old SATA drives, making the system boot and apps load quickly. Look for NVMe (PCIe 4.0/5.0) for speed and check endurance (TBW) for long-term use.

  • PSU (Power Supply Unit)

    The PSU provides stable power to every component. Choose one with enough wattage for your CPU+GPU, an 80 Plus efficiency rating (Bronze/Silver/Gold+), and good protections. Modular cables help keep your build tidy.

  • Air Cooler

    An air cooler uses a heatsink and fan to move heat away from the CPU. It's reliable, quiet on many models, and great for most builds, especially mid- range systems that don't need extreme cooling.

  • AIO Cooler (All In One)

    An AIO is a closed-loop liquid cooler: a pump, radiator, and fans in one package. It can cool high-performance CPUs better and often looks cleaner, but costs more and requires space for the radiator.
